Problem Framing

Before solving, learn to ask what the problem really is.

Schools often hand students already-defined problems. Real life rarely does. The deepest learning often begins one step earlier.

What if defining the problem is more important than solving it?

Real problems are messy

In the world outside the classroom, problems are poorly defined, politically loaded, scientifically uncertain, and shaped by competing values. People disagree not only about solutions, but about what the problem even is.

Framing is a form of thinking

Problem framing asks students to identify stakeholders, boundaries, uncertainty, values, and hidden assumptions. It slows down the rush toward answers and makes the structure of a situation visible.

What is the real problem? What matters? What is uncertain? Who is affected? These questions are not delays. They are part of serious thought.

Better questions change what becomes possible

When AI can produce answers quickly, the advantage shifts toward people who can ask better questions, notice what is missing, and decide what kind of problem is worth solving in the first place.
