About This Journey

From Modelling Forests to Modelling Learning

A short journey through the experiences that shaped this work.

Research

My scientific work has focused on complex earth systems—climate, forests, insects, carbon cycles, and the disturbances that reshape ecosystems over time. These are systems that cannot be understood through simple cause and effect.

Research taught me to live with uncertainty: incomplete data, competing explanations, and problems that shift as you try to define them.

Modelling

Models became my primary way of thinking. Not as polished final products, but as working structures—assumptions made visible, relationships named, ideas made testable and revisable.

Modelling is how messy reality becomes something you can reason with, challenge, and improve.

Teaching

Alongside research, I began asking a parallel question: could these ways of thinking become accessible to young people—not as career training, but as a form of intellectual practice?

Teaching, for me, is less about delivering content and more about designing experiences where students learn to frame, represent, and judge.

Field Notes

Years in research environments left a residue of observation: how scientists actually work when problems are open-ended, when data is incomplete, when models fail and must be rebuilt.

These field notes—lessons from practice, not theory—are what I draw on when imagining new forms of learning.

Education

The question that now organizes my work is educational: how should young people learn to think when AI can generate answers, when complexity is unavoidable, and when judgment matters more than recall?

I explore learning through modelling as one possible bridge—from scientific practice to classroom inquiry, from systems research to systems thinking for the next generation.
